Clark was playing guitar shirtless, so I threw him one and said lets go to the party now, so we went. I don’t remember much of the party, but I remember only seeing a few glimpses of Clark and the other guys, and none of them were at the after party.

      The next day, when I showed up, they were in Clark’s room, sitting around chugging beers. We were planning on tailgating UVA’s football game; not going to the game, just drinking beforehand at the fraternity houses. They were going over the night before. Jimmy was telling Moondog and Vic Fox about how Clark had shot-gunned a few beers at the Sig Chi house then disappeared. Clark’s friend Jimmy had made out with and fingered a girl on the dance floor. Moondog and Vic Fox said that they also made out with and fingered girls on the dance floor. Then Jimmy said that when he had to leave he couldn’t find Clark, then somebody said he’s passed out in the bathroom. Jimmy found him sitting on the toilet with his pants down, sleeping like a baby. He woke him up and they left the party immediately and got on the first wagon that passed by. They sat in the back. On the wagon, Clark said he didn’t feel so good. He stood up as if to move to get out of the bus, then grabbed a seat on each side of the aisle and threw up a torrent of orange all down the middle of the bus. People screamed.
      Jimmy, Vic Fox and Moondog all cracked up. Clark had said ‘sorry’ to the driver as he got off at his stop, but the driver just shook his head.
They all drank a beer and I got a beer. Jimmy and Clark told stories about growing up in Virginia Beach, football, lacrosse, surfing in the winter. They made V.B. sound a lot better than it really is. Fox told a story about the swell of the decade in Tampa Bay and they all made farting noises and threw beer on him. Finally, we got up and hopped on a wagon to go to the tailgates. After seriously thirty seconds, Clark said “oh no”, leaned over me, and threw up all over the aisle. I screamed and pushed him away. Other people on the aisle screamed also. Clark yelled an apology to the bus driver. When we got to the tailgate, Clark swished beer in his mouth, spit it out, then tried kiss me! Anyway, he made out with some other girl, and then sure enough, on the way home he puked on the girl’s dress.
      Little did we know, but Clark’s motion sickness was here to stay. Christmas break came and went, we got new classes in the spring and new parties to go to, but Clark’s motion sickness persisted. And although he didn’t throw up every time he went on a wagon, or even every time he drank and went on a wagon, he did puke enough times to get a reputation among the wagon divers, and some would laugh with him, some would ask him to sit near the door or the trash, and the graver ones would not even allow him on the wagon, so he would have to wait for the next one or walk.

      At the beginning of second semester Clark’s advisor, some lady he had talked to once, said he had to take at least two General Education classes and one had to be a writing class. Roost and Moondog had similar requirements to fill, so the three of them signed up for Intro to Poetry, the easiest writing class, and Macroeconomics, which was taught by the supposed best teacher at UVA, Dr. Alvaro Chuckles. I didn’t sign up for either. Roost and Clark ended up in the same classes for both, but Moondog got screwed with an eight a.m. poetry class.
      Anyway, on the first day of classes second semester, I came to Clark’s room and smoked a little with him and Roost and then Moondog showed up and said eight a.m. poetry was not bad, there was this smoking hot girl with a French last name in his class, along with four other girls who were decent looking, and the class had read Dylan Thomas then called it a day.

“Who’s your teacher?” Clark asked.
“Some fat asshole who wore sun glasses the whole time,” Moondog replied.
“Same,” said Clark, “Kosner.”
      Clark told us that Kosner, who was wearing a purple sweater that barely covered his gut while standing, had sat Indian style on top of his desk, showing nearly half his ass-crack to anyone on the sides of the room. He was sweating and had, in his deep Mississippi accent, cursed non-stop for a minute while explaining “The Road Not Taken”. Clark also mentioned a girl named Restin Lewis in his class, who was an east coast surfer like them, but she was from Jacksonville, which had possibly the worst waves on the east coast.
“Its no Bay Head,” said Moondog.
“She said she used to do some competitions,” continued Clark, “but she was never sponsored, like me. Regardless, she’s a babe.”

      Then they talked about Macroeconomics and Roost said that Professor Chuckles was a genius, an absolute genius, and Clark said that all the talk about supply and demand made him feel like he was going to be learning magic powers.
      “Chuckles himself is a little squirt of a guy though isn’t he?” said Clark, “I bet Kosner would fuck him up in a fight.”
      “I wouldn’t trust Kosner’s heart,” said Moondog, “if the going got tough, he would give up like a little bitch.”
      Two weeks later, Clark had thrown his Economics textbook out the window and was reading Poe night and day. It was hard to keep track of him, he started studying in the library more and sleeping until noon, and when he went to parties he would only show up really late. Once, he invited Restin over to smoke and beforehand asked us not to say anything about how bizarre he had been acting or about puking on the wagons. Then she showed up, and she was cute, it was my first time meeting her: long brown hair with natural highlights, tan, pretty tiny but toned. Great ass. So we smoked and talked about sororities and parties, then Clark brought up poetry and Roost did his impression of Kosner leaning back with his feet up the on desk, stomach hanging out from under his cardigan sweater. We all laughed, and then they started talking about politics! Gag me with a spoon. But Roost and Restin got a little heated, and Clark and Moondog sided with her and then Roost got up and left. Then Restin said bye in her adorable way and I didn’t see her again until Pontooning.

      After that, Clark basically stopped smiling and I seriously didn’t see him for like two weeks. When I finally did, he was restlessly picking at his guitar. I knew he was just working on a song, but I know Clark, and I could tell that something else was going on, beside the fact that he couldn’t ride on vans, or concentrate on school, or be anywhere on time, or that he didn’t care about me or anyone else anymore. We were alone in his room and it was cold and gray outside the window. I looked at him, I was probably crying a little bit, and asked, “What happened to you Clark?”
      He stopped playing and looked up at me. He looked calm, almost normal, but he took a deep breath and let it out slowly, then said, “Cherry, don’t tell anyone this, but I think I might be losing it.”
      At that moment there was a strong draft through the window and three seconds later Clark’s door slammed closed. Clark’s eyes were fixed on his poster of Jimi Hendrix on the wall, which had almost been blown off by the gust of wind.
      “I don’t know what is happening to me,” Clark said, still looking at the poster, “it’s like I’m not in control anymore. My stomach. My heart. When I walk down the hallway I feel like I am walking a balance beam. My stomach is in my mouth and my heart is shaking. Even sitting here now, it is like I am in this chair sitting next to the precipice. I look around and it is all air and rocks far below, and I am on a little outcropping on the side of a cliff. The world is miniature and immense below me. Soon, the cliff will collapse. Or worse, I could gather up the courage or cowardice to throw myself off. I just keep telling myself to back away, for the precipice to go away.”
      Then he looked at me and asked for me to please let him explain. I, of course, said there was nothing I would rather do than listen to him talk.
      He continued, “My mind has always been a little all over the place. You know I get angry, but I love to laugh; I am quick to fight, quick to forgive. Things seemed like that at first, until this precipice started setting in. Now I am just waiting for it to appear, all the time. When I feel O.K. I can think or do some work or relax, a little bit. But I am waiting for it to show up. And in class? Forget about it. Chuckles class is torture, everything he says and everybody in there makes me feel like I am already in free fall, like I can’t catch my breath because the wind in my face is so strong. So I don’t go. And I only go to Kosner because I have to, and the things he says and Restin says and the dead poets seem to help, but overall it is uncomfortable. And my family. At the beginning of the semester, Christmas break, seeing my family and friends only made things worse. The first time I felt the precipice was after drinking a cup of coffee with my friends at the diner. I had to get up and leave. So here I am. Me by myself.

      But I think it’s just college, not being away from home or the stress of schoolwork, but I feel like people are making decisions for me, not the universe, not myself, but other people. They tell me what to study and that this is how I make my life. And at the same time I have all the choice in the world in terms of meeting new people, meeting new girls, living my day-to-day life. If I want to go to the park or play basketball or fuck this girl I just met, everybody is fine with it. But if I want to study anthropology instead of economics everybody calls me a pussy or a waster. So I toe the line while the world spins around me. I feel like I am on a conveyor belt being drawn toward some human determined end piece while the world around me dances and changes glitteringly fast. I need a point on the horizon or near me to fix my gaze. I need to stand still for a second, to breath deep and listen to the sound of water on rocks. How can school help me with this? The only air and water is pumped in through lead pipes. And the things that used to come naturally to me, meeting good people, making enduring friendships, playing music, playing sports, being curious, taking chances, these things have all been shoved out of sight or tucked into the corners. I love you Cherry, but we were bad together, we did not make each other better. We met at some horrible party and the love between us was shared with booze and loud music. And drinking is barely fun anymore. We’ll always be friends though, and the close friends I have made here are the only ones I can see these days. The parties and tons of strangers and fake friends, when I am with them its is like another person joins the crowd, some demon who makes me feel like I am nothing and I have no face and words become unintelligible. Or even worse, when I am with them, a crowd, a few people, it does not matter, I feel like I am an explorer on an unknown island. I step off my canoe and onto the sand. A silent ring of savages surrounds me. They have dark brown skin and are covered in red paint. I am a savage too. I have dark brown skin covered in blue paint. They are pointing spears at me, and bows and arrows. There are women and children in the crowd also. They start to talk. They speak a language I do not understand. I speak some words and they yell to drown me out. I do some gestures with my hands and my body, to show them I do not wish them any harm, that I am just passing through. They look at me with alien eyes. I could not possibly imagine what they are thinking. For all I know, they are cannibals.”

      At this point he stopped and looked at me in silence. Obviously, I had started to cry when he said we were bad together.
      He went on, “You know love has something to do with it, or the absence of love. Love in everything, in the doing and the sacrifice and the little things that fill the time in between them. But this is where I feel completely stuck. I can’t see the beauty in a poem or an equation or in another person when all signs seem to indicate that I am a unit to be processed by the school and spat out, ready to be gobbled up by the world or to eek out a way of owning my life through loans and mortgages. There is still some love for me in music though. And you know I like Restin, but I can’t even get to know her outside of class because I am such a shell. I see endless opportunities to live, but the inertia here is crushing me. I might have to leave school and get a job. But if I get a job I might have to go on strike. And then go on strike from the strike.”
      “And I used to be such an agreeable guy,” he said to me with a smile. He looked out the window, and then, softly spoke, “I need some way to the surface. I need the universe to save me.”

      As the semester went on nothing seemed to change for Clark. We saw him more, but that was just because he stopped going to the library and spent more time in his room and in bed, and he still went to parties but it never looked like he was having fun, except for a brief period when he was very drunk but not yet totally incoherent. By the end of the semester, I honestly avoided going to his room because I still had feelings for him and it hurt to see him like that, and to not be with him or be able to help him.
      But the last week of the semester, in early May, I popped in to see what was going on, and found Clark, Roost and Moondog talking about a confrontation Clark had just had with Professor Chuckles during office hours. Clark had shown up to ask about opportunities to salvage his grade and possibly pass the class. Chuckles asked him why he struggled with the material while many of his peers seemed to enjoy the class and were doing well. At that point, Clark basically admitted that he thought the class was bullshit and that economics classes were herding his peers like cattle to jobs in banking and consulting and marketing, where they would be abused like cattle, while only a few truly understood and respected the discipline. Chuckles cut him off and said there were no opportunities for extra credit and for Clark to leave his office immediately, then Clark said “don’t be short with me”, and continued his tirade against teaching people the marginal return of eating a orange. Finally, Chuckles told Clark to study up good for the final, if he got above a B he could pass the class, and that in the future, he should stay away from any courses that could lead to an important or significant job.
      We all laughed. Clark said it felt great to throw some tack back at Chuckles after listening to the man preach and be praised by dweebs for fourteen weeks. Then Roost told everyone that, by surprise, he would be passing Poetry class, even thought Kosner had written very offensive remarks on all his papers and homeworks. For the final essay, Roost wrote a twelve-page paper on Paradise Lost and got a D-. Then Clark said that he had written a twenty-five-page, stream of consciousness explorative memoir of his thoughts and feelings for the last month, and got a B. Finally, Moondog said he wrote a six page paper on “In the desert” by Stephan Crane and had gotten an A+.
      “Fuck you,” said Roost.
      “Restin got an A+ on her paper too,” said Clark, “Then she got up and read this dope poem. The first few lines went:

The flesh is sad—and I’ve read every book.
O to escape—to get away. Birds look
as though they’re drunk for unknown spray and skies.

      And then some other stuff.”

      “Read all the books?” said Roost, “Impossible.”
      “Anyway,” continued Clark, “after class I ran down Kosner as he speed-walked to his car. I asked him if there was any point to majoring in Literature and he said yes, of course, you learn critical reading and writing skills that can help you in many careers. Then I asked him what he had done with his degree and he said he had been a litigator for twenty-five years. I shook his hand, hopped on a UVA wagon headed back to the dorms, and threw up all over the back seat.”

      Afterward, we had finals week, and a few hours before I drove back to D.C. I stopped into Clark’s room for the last time and found him sitting on his bed while Vic Fox and Moondog broke up weed for a blunt.
      “I am sorry to hear about what happened at your final,” I said to Clark. Everyone had heard about Clark showing up late for the Econ final and Chuckles announcing to the class that Clark was the worst student ever.
      “Thanks, Cherry,” said Clark, looking at me with a smile, “I actually saw him later that day, totally, by accident, I was walking to get some tacos and he was hailing a cab, and when the cab driver got there I heard Chuckles just explode on the guy, something about being late and how important Chuckles flight to Connecticut was. Chuckles was so livid that he didn’t notice me walking up to him and the cabbie, and I got in between them, stared Chuckles dead in the eye, and said, ‘Who are you? Who are you to curse this guy off and compose yourself with no decency or respect? Who the fuck are you?!”

“Oh my god,” I replied, “What did Chuckles say?”
“He called me a little shit, sneered, and got in the cab.”
“Well, I guess that’s good.”
“Yea,” said Clark, “So want to come smoke this blunt with us? Last one of the year.”
“No thanks, I have to get going.”
      I gave him a hug, and as I walked away he said, “Hey Cherry, thanks for burning me that Leonard Cohen CD.”

“Yea, its my favorite”.
“See you next year.”
